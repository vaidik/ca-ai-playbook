## Prompt 1 — Demand Notice Response Draft

**When to use:** A client receives an Income Tax demand notice and you need to draft a formal reply to the department.

**Time saved:** 45–60 minutes per notice → 5 minutes with this prompt.

---

**THE PROMPT:**

```
You are a senior Chartered Accountant helping draft a formal response
to an Income Tax demand notice.

Client name: [NAME]
Assessment Year: [AY e.g. AY 2023-24]
Notice type: [e.g. 143(1) intimation / 148 reassessment / 245 adjustment]
Demand amount: ₹[AMOUNT]
Reason for demand as per notice: [PASTE TEXT FROM NOTICE OR SUMMARISE IN 2-3 LINES]
Our position / reason to contest: [EXPLAIN CLIENT'S SIDE IN 2-3 LINES]

Draft a formal reply letter to the Assessing Officer.
Tone: professional, factual, firm.
Format: proper salutation, clear subject line, numbered points,
request for demand to be dropped / rectified.
End with a request for a hearing date if applicable.
```

---

**EXAMPLE OUTPUT:**

Subject: Response to Intimation u/s 143(1) for AY 2023-24 — PAN: ABCDE1234F

To,
The Assessing Officer,
Income Tax Ward [X], [City]

Sir/Madam,

We write in response to the intimation dated [DATE] raising a demand of ₹1,24,500 for AY 2023-24 in the matter of M/s ABC Traders (PAN: ABCDE1234F).

**1. Nature of Demand**
The demand appears to have been raised on account of a mismatch between TDS credit claimed in the return and credit reflecting in Form 26AS at the time of processing.

**2. Our Position**
The TDS of ₹1,24,500 deducted by [Deductor Name] has since been reflected in the updated Form 26AS/AIS as of [DATE]. The mismatch was a processing delay and does not reflect any actual tax liability.

**3. Relief Sought**
We respectfully request that the demand be reduced to nil upon verification of the enclosed Form 26AS / TDS reconciliation. Should any discrepancy remain, we request an opportunity to be heard before any coercive action.

Enclosures: Form 26AS extract, TDS certificates (Form 16A), Copy of ITR acknowledgement

Yours faithfully,
[CA Name], FCA
[Firm Name | Membership No.]

## Prompt 2 — Tax Advisory Email to Client

**When to use:** Explaining a tax position, planning opportunity, or regulatory change to a client in plain language.

---

**THE PROMPT:**

```
You are a Chartered Accountant writing an advisory email to a client.
Write in clear, simple English — no jargon unless necessary,
and explain any technical terms you use.

Client type: [salaried individual / business owner / HUF / company director]
Advisory topic: [e.g. new tax regime vs old regime / ESOP taxation /
selling residential property / switching to presumptive taxation]

Key points to cover:
1. [Point 1]
2. [Point 2]
3. [Point 3]

Also mention: any deadline or action required from the client,
and what documents/information you need from them.

Format: professional email, warm but not informal, 3–5 short paragraphs.
```

---

**EXAMPLE OUTPUT:**

Dear Ramesh,

I hope all's well. Following our call last week, I wanted to summarise the tax implications of switching to the new tax regime for FY 2025-26 so you have everything in writing before we finalise your declaration with HR.

Under the new regime, your tax on ₹24L income works out to approximately ₹3.9L before cess — versus ₹4.1L under the old regime after your current deductions (80C + HRA + NPS). The new regime saves you roughly ₹20,000 this year. That said, this only works in your favour because your HRA claim is limited. If you shift to a higher-rent arrangement or increase NPS, the old regime could swing back.

**What I need from you:** Confirm whether your rent amount is changing this year, and whether you plan to continue the NPS top-up. Reply by April 30th so I can advise HR before the April payroll.

Happy to jump on a call if anything's unclear.

Warm regards,
[CA Name]

## Prompt 11 — Audit Observation (Memo Style)

**When to use:** Writing a clean audit observation for inclusion in an internal or management report.

---

**THE PROMPT:**

```
Write a formal audit observation in the style used in statutory audit reports.

Observation title: [e.g. "Unreconciled Creditor Balances"]
Area: [Accounts Payable / Revenue / Fixed Assets / Payroll / etc.]
Finding: [DESCRIBE WHAT YOU FOUND]
Amount / instances involved: [₹AMOUNT or NUMBER OF INSTANCES]
Risk / implication: [WHY THIS MATTERS]
Recommendation: [WHAT THE COMPANY SHOULD DO]

Format: standard audit memo structure —
Observation → Impact → Root Cause (if known) → Recommendation →
Management Response (leave blank for client to fill).
Professional, no unnecessary words.
```

---

**EXAMPLE OUTPUT:**

**Observation: Trade Payables Not Confirmed via Balance Confirmation**

**Area:** Accounts Payable

**Finding:** Of 48 trade creditors with balances over ₹1L as at 31 March 2024, balance confirmation letters were sent to 31 parties. Responses were received from only 14. The remaining 17 creditors (aggregate balance: ₹34.7L) have not been confirmed.

**Impact:** Unconfirmed balances present a risk of over/understatement of liabilities. Without independent corroboration, reliance is placed solely on the company's books.

**Root Cause:** Balance confirmation process was initiated mid-April rather than the recommended February timeline.

**Recommendation:** Initiate the balance confirmation process by January 31st each year. Document a formal follow-up protocol with a second reminder and escalation to senior management.

**Management Response:** [To be filled by management]

## Prompt 31 — LinkedIn Post (Educational Tax Tip)

**When to use:** Building your LinkedIn presence as a CA by sharing useful, accessible content.

---

**THE PROMPT:**

```
Write a LinkedIn post from the perspective of an Indian Chartered Accountant
sharing a useful tax tip or insight.

Topic: [e.g. "5 things salaried employees forget before submitting their
investment declaration" / "Why your GST ITC might get rejected" /
"The new rule on TDS on rent every landlord should know"]

Format:
- Hook in the first line (no "I'm happy to share" openers — get to the point)
- 4–6 short, scannable bullet points or numbered steps
- Practical, specific, actionable
- Closing line that invites engagement or offers help
- 3–4 relevant hashtags at the end
- Max 250 words

Tone: expert but accessible. You're helping, not lecturing.
```

---

**EXAMPLE OUTPUT:**

Your Form 16 doesn't tell the whole story.

4 things your employer can't capture — that you need to declare yourself:

1. **Interest on savings account** — Even ₹10,000 is taxable. Check all accounts, including dormant ones.
2. **Rent income** — If you've let out a property (even to family), it needs to be declared.
3. **Capital gains** — Mutual fund redemptions, stock sales, property sold — your employer has no visibility on this.
4. **Freelance income** — Side projects, consulting, referral fees — all taxable, even if received in cash.

Most of these are simple to declare. The risk of not doing it isn't worth it.

## A Note on Using AI Responsibly

These prompts produce first drafts. You are the professional.

- Review everything before sending or filing
- AI doesn't know your client's specific facts unless you tell it
- Never paste confidential client data into a public AI tool — use general descriptions or anonymised details
- Cross-check any legal or regulatory references against primary sources

The goal is to get to a good first draft faster. The professional judgement is still yours.
